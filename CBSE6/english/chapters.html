Chapter 1
A Bottle of Dew
Unit 1: Fables and Folk Tales — A folk tale by Sudha Murty about a lazy landowner who learns the value of hard work through an unlikely lesson from a wise sage.
📖 Summary

Rama Natha inherited large tracts of land but completely neglected them. He was obsessed with finding a magic potion that could turn ordinary objects into gold. Many people cheated him by selling fake potions. His sensible wife Madhumati worried they would go bankrupt.

One day, a wise sage named Mahipati visited their village. Rama Natha begged him for the secret of making gold. The sage told him to plant banana trees on his land, tend them carefully, and collect 5 litres of morning dew from the banana leaves. He said this would take about 6 years.

Rama Natha worked tirelessly for 6 years — planting, watering, and collecting dew every morning. When he finally brought the bottle of dew to the sage, Mahipati muttered a chant over it and told Rama to sprinkle it on a copper vessel. Nothing happened. Rama was furious.

But Madhumati then showed him a box full of gold coins. While Rama had been busy collecting dew, she had been selling the bananas from the thriving plantation all along. The sage smiled and revealed the real lesson: hard work, not magic, creates true wealth.

👤 Characters
  • ●Rama Natha — A lazy landowner who inherited large tracts of land but neglected them, obsessed with finding a magic potion to turn objects into gold.
  • ●Madhumati — Rama Natha’s sensible and hardworking wife who quietly sold the bananas from the plantation and saved the earnings.
  • ●Sage Mahipati — A wise sage who cleverly tricked Rama Natha into working hard by giving him the task of collecting dew from banana leaves.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: The value of hard work over shortcuts and magic. The story shows how a clever sage used Rama Natha’s greed to channel him into productive labour.

Moral: Hard work creates real wealth, not magic or shortcuts. There is no substitute for honest effort and dedication.

Sub-themes: The wisdom of patience, the role of a supportive partner (Madhumati), the danger of greed and gullibility.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
PotionA liquid mixture believed to have magical powers
TendedCared for; looked after
MutteredSpoke in a low, barely audible voice
PreciousOf great value; very important
WisdomThe quality of having good judgement and experience
ChantA repeated phrase or words spoken rhythmically
DewTiny drops of water that form on cool surfaces at night
Large tracts of landBig areas or stretches of land
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Homophones: Words that sound the same but have different meanings and spellings — I/eye, dew/due, son/sun. Practice using them correctly in sentences.
  • ✎Connecting Words: Words that join ideas in sentences — because, before, as soon as, as. Example: “As soon as the sage arrived, Rama Natha begged for help.”
  • ✎Past Tense Narration: The story is told in the simple past tense. Identify verbs like inherited, neglected, planted, collected, revealed.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The sage’s task of collecting dew was not magical — it was a clever trick to make Rama Natha work on his land for 6 years.
  • ★Madhumati is the real hero — she quietly sold the bananas and saved the gold coins while her husband chased magic.
  • ★The story is by Sudha Murty, a well-known Indian author known for her folk tale collections.
  • ★Key homophones from this chapter: dew/due, I/eye, son/sun — commonly tested in exams.
  • ★The moral can be summarised as: “There is no shortcut to success — only hard work pays.”
Chapter 2
The Raven and the Fox
Unit 1: Fables and Folk Tales — A classic poem by Jean de La Fontaine about a cunning fox who uses flattery to trick a proud raven.
📖 Summary

Mr Raven sat perched on a limb of a tree, holding a morsel of food in his big beak. Reynard the Fox spotted him from below and decided to get the food through cunning flattery.

The Fox praised the Raven lavishly — calling him a handsome bird with magnificent feathers. Then he added: “If you could sing as well as you look, you’d surely be the King of the woods!”

The Raven, swelling with pride, forgot that his voice was nothing but a croak. He opened his beak wide to sing — and the morsel of food fell straight down. The Fox grabbed it instantly and laughed.

The Fox’s parting lesson: “Ignore sweet words that make you glow. Pride is rather unwise, you should know.”

📌 Key Points
  • ●Form: A narrative poem with 4 stanzas and an AABB rhyme scheme (couplets).
  • ●Style: Classic Aesop-style fable retold in verse by the French poet Jean de La Fontaine.
  • ●Mr Raven — A proud bird who is easily flattered and loses his food.
  • ●Reynard the Fox — A cunning, smooth-talking fox who uses flattery to get what he wants.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: The dangers of pride and flattery. The poem illustrates how vanity makes people vulnerable to manipulation.

Moral: Don’t let pride and flattery fool you. Those who flatter often have selfish motives.

Sub-themes: Cunning vs. innocence, the gap between appearance and reality, the cost of vanity.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
PerchedSat or rested on a high or narrow place
MorselA small piece of food
SeekTo look for; to try to find
PrideAn excessively high opinion of oneself
EyedLooked at closely or with interest
LimbA large branch of a tree
WoodsA small forest; an area with many trees
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Alliteration: Repetition of initial consonant sounds — “big beak”, “song of sweet.” Identify more examples in the poem.
  • ✎Rhyming Words: Pairs from the poem — limb/him, word/bird, joke/croak, know/glow. The AABB scheme means consecutive lines rhyme.
  • ✎Opposites: Key antonym pairs — perched/flew, forgot/remembered, foolish/wise.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The poem follows the AABB rhyme scheme (couplets) — each pair of consecutive lines rhymes.
  • ★The Fox’s strategy was flattery — he praised the Raven’s looks to make him open his beak.
  • ★The Raven’s mistake was pride — he believed the Fox’s false praise and forgot his voice was a croak.
  • ★This is an Aesop-style fable retold in verse by the French poet Jean de La Fontaine.
  • ★For exams, be ready to identify alliteration, rhyming words and the moral of the poem.
Chapter 3
The Unlikely Best Friends
Unit 2: Friendship — A prose piece by Subba Rao about the heartwarming bond between a royal elephant and a stray dog.
📖 Summary

Gajaraj, a royal elephant, lived in grand stables but was lonely despite all the care he received. One day, a stray dog named Buntee wandered into the stables. Against all expectations, the two became the best of friends.

They shared everything — Gajaraj would drop food crumbs for Buntee, they played together, and Buntee even accompanied Gajaraj during his baths, splashing and plunging in the water alongside him.

One day, a farmer recognised Buntee as his lost dog and took him away. After that, both Gajaraj and Buntee stopped eating. The elephant refused food and stood listlessly; Buntee yelped and winced at the farmer’s home.

When the farmer saw Buntee’s deep sadness and heard about the elephant’s condition, he brought the dog back to the stables. Both friends were reunited with great joy. The mahout and the farmer became friends too — a friendship born from watching the bond between their animals.

👤 Characters
  • ●Gajaraj — A royal elephant who lived in grand stables but was lonely until Buntee arrived.
  • ●Buntee — A stray dog who wandered into the stables and became Gajaraj’s inseparable friend.
  • ●The Mahout — Gajaraj’s caretaker who looked after the elephant in the royal stables.
  • ●The Farmer — Buntee’s original owner who took him away but eventually returned him after seeing the dog’s sadness.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: True friendship transcends all differences — size, species and background do not matter when there is genuine affection.

Moral: Friendship knows no boundaries. The bond between Gajaraj and Buntee shows that love and companionship can form between the most unlikely pairs.

Sub-themes: Loneliness and the need for companionship, the emotional lives of animals, how friendships between animals can inspire friendships between humans.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
StablesA building where horses or elephants are kept
StrayedWandered away from where one belongs
MunchingEating steadily with a crunching sound
WaggedMoved (a tail) from side to side rapidly
CrumbsSmall fragments of food
AccompaniedWent along with; joined
PlungingJumping or diving quickly into water
YelpedMade a short, sharp cry (of a dog)
WincedFlinched; drew back in pain or distress
RelievedFeeling reassured after anxiety or worry
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Opposites with Prefixes: Adding prefixes to make antonyms — un- (unlikely, unhappy), dis- (disagree, disappear), mis- (misunderstand, misbehave).
  • ✎Past Tense Forms: Simple Past (wandered, shared, stopped), Past Progressive (was eating, were playing), Past Perfect (had become, had taken).
  • ✎Neither…nor / Either…or: Correlative conjunctions — “Neither Gajaraj nor Buntee would eat.” “Either the farmer returns Buntee or the elephant will fall ill.”
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The friendship between Gajaraj and Buntee is “unlikely” because of their vast difference in size and species.
  • ★Both animals stopped eating when separated — this shows the depth of their emotional bond.
  • ★The story inspired a human friendship too — the mahout and the farmer became friends.
  • ★Key grammar: practise forming opposites using prefixes (un-, dis-, mis-) and using neither…nor / either…or.
  • ★For comprehension, focus on why both animals stopped eating and how the problem was resolved.
Chapter 4
A Friend’s Prayer
Unit 2: Friendship — A heartfelt poem by Jill Wolf about unconditional friendship, empathy and accepting friends as they are.
📖 Summary

In this gentle, prayer-like poem, the speaker expresses her deep wish that friendship will always remain the most important thing in her life. She feels blessed to have special friends and wants to give her very best to them.

The speaker prays for the ability to use her heart — not just her eyes — to truly understand what friends can be. She wants to make no judgements, to love her friends exactly as they are, and to be worthy of the friendships she has been given.

The poem is written as 5 couplets (pairs of lines) in a sincere, reflective tone. It emphasises that true friendship requires constant effort, empathy, and the willingness to accept people without conditions.

📌 Key Points
  • ●Form: 5 couplets with a prayer-like, reflective tone.
  • ●Speaker: A person expressing gratitude for friends and praying for wisdom to be a better friend.
  • ●Central idea: Use your heart (not just eyes) to understand friends; love them without judgement.
  • ●Tone: Warm, sincere, humble and grateful.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: Unconditional love and acceptance in friendship. The poem celebrates friendship as something sacred that requires empathy and effort.

Moral: True friendship requires empathy, effort, and accepting friends as they are — without judgement or conditions.

Sub-themes: Gratitude for friends, the importance of seeing with the heart, self-improvement for the sake of relationships.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
BlessedFortunate; feeling grateful for something good
PrayerA sincere request or expression of hope
JudgementsOpinions formed about someone, often without full understanding
RealiseTo become aware of; to understand clearly
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Qualities of Friends: Adjectives that describe good friends — helpful, loyal, kind, caring, honest, supportive, trustworthy.
  • ✎Synonyms: Words with similar meanings — blessed = grateful = thankful. Practice finding synonyms for other words in the poem.
  • ✎Prayer/Wish Sentences: The poem uses structures expressing hope — “I pray that…”, “I want to…”. These use subjunctive-like constructions.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The poem’s key message: use your heart, not just your eyes, to understand what friends truly are.
  • ★The speaker wants to make no judgements and love friends as they are — this is unconditional friendship.
  • ★The poem has 5 couplets (pairs of rhyming lines) and is written like a prayer.
  • ★Synonyms of “blessed”: grateful, thankful, fortunate — commonly asked in exams.
  • ★For writing tasks, you may be asked to write about your best friend using qualities discussed in this poem.
Chapter 5
Neem Baba
Unit 3: Nurturing Nature — A dialogue-format prose piece by S. I. Farooqi where a curious girl talks to a personified neem tree about its many gifts.
📖 Summary

In this unique dialogue-format story, a curious girl named Amber talks to a neem tree that has been personified as “Neem Baba.” The tree tells her its fascinating history — it was born millions of years ago in North India and Myanmar and has since spread worldwide.

Neem Baba shares its Sanskrit names: Arishta (reliever of sickness), Nimba and Nimbaca. It describes its countless uses: twigs for cleaning teeth, leaves for protecting clothes from insects, seed powder for protecting crops and grains, oil for making soaps and toothpaste, and all its parts for traditional medicine.

The tree also explains that its wood is resistant to termites, making it ideal for furniture, and that it purifies the air around it. Neem Baba gives endlessly without asking for anything in return.

Through this conversation, the chapter teaches respect and gratitude for nature, showing how a single tree can serve humanity in dozens of ways.

👤 Characters
  • ●Amber — A curious and respectful girl who asks Neem Baba questions about its history and uses.
  • ●Neem Baba — A personified neem tree that shares its ancient history, Sanskrit names and countless uses with Amber.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: Respect and gratitude for nature. The neem tree symbolises selfless giving — it provides medicine, wood, oil, air purification and more without asking anything in return.

Moral: Nature gives us everything we need. We must respect, protect and be grateful for trees and the natural world.

Sub-themes: Personification as a storytelling device, traditional knowledge about plants, biodiversity, environmental awareness.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
Thought-provokingMaking you think carefully about something
GraceElegance and beauty of form or movement
MeaslesAn infectious disease causing red spots on the skin
LocustsLarge insects that travel in swarms and destroy crops
TermitesInsects that eat and destroy wood
StagnantNot flowing or moving; still and often dirty (water)
Cost-effectiveGiving good value for the money spent
PurifiesMakes clean; removes impurities
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Compound Words: Open (neem oil, well known), Closed (something, childhood), Hyphenated (thought-provoking, cost-effective). Learn to identify and form each type.
  • ✎Personification: The neem tree is given human qualities — it speaks, shares memories, and teaches. This literary device makes nature relatable.
  • ✎Present-to-Past Verb Forms: Practice converting present tense verbs to past tense — purifies → purified, protects → protected, gives → gave.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The neem tree’s Sanskrit names: Arishta (reliever of sickness), Nimba and Nimbaca.
  • ★Uses of neem: teeth cleaning (twigs), clothes protection (leaves), crop protection (seed powder), soaps/toothpaste (oil), medicine (all parts), furniture (termite-resistant wood).
  • ★The chapter uses personification — the tree talks like a wise elder, hence “Neem Baba.”
  • ★Three types of compound words: open (two separate words), closed (one combined word), hyphenated (joined by a hyphen).
  • ★The neem tree originated in North India and Myanmar millions of years ago and has since spread worldwide.
Chapter 6
What a Bird Thought
Unit 3: Nurturing Nature — An anonymous poem about a bird’s growing understanding of the world as it moves from egg to nest to tree to sky.
📖 Summary

A bird narrates how its understanding of the world changed as it grew. In the first stanza, the bird lay inside a pale blue egg and thought the whole world was a small, round shell.

In the second stanza, the bird hatched and found itself in a straw nest with its mother. Now the world seemed to be made of straw — warm and safe.

In the third stanza, the bird hopped onto a tree branch and saw green leaves all around. It thought the world was made of leaves, and admitted: “I have been very blind.”

In the final stanza, the bird flew beyond the tree into the open sky. Now it realised it still didn’t know how the world was truly made — and admitted humbly: “I don’t know how the world is made, and neither do my neighbours.”

📌 Key Points
  • ●Form: 4 stanzas with an ABCB rhyme scheme (second and fourth lines rhyme).
  • ●Narrator: A bird that grows from an egg to a flying bird, each stage changing its view of the world.
  • ●Progression: Egg (shell) → Nest (straw) → Branch (leaves) → Sky (unknown — humility).
  • ●Tone: Reflective, honest, and increasingly humble.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: Knowledge grows with experience. Each new experience expands our understanding, but true wisdom lies in knowing how much we still don’t know.

Moral: Stay humble about what you know. The more you learn, the more you realise how vast the world truly is.

Sub-themes: Growth and perspective, the limits of knowledge, intellectual humility, the beauty of discovery.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
PaleLight in colour; not bright
FlutteredMoved wings quickly and lightly
LabourHard work; effort
NestledSettled comfortably and snugly
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Rhyming Words: Pairs from the poem — well/shell, other/mother, find/blind, labours/neighbours. The poem follows an ABCB rhyme scheme.
  • ✎Describing Words (Adjectives): Notice the adjectives used — pointed, green, thin, brown, small, wooden, hanging, round, pale. These paint vivid pictures.
  • ✎Progressive Understanding: The bird uses the phrase “I thought” repeatedly, showing how perspective changes with experience.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The bird’s journey: Egg → Nest → Branch → Sky — each stage expands its understanding of the world.
  • ★The final stanza is the most important: the bird admits “I don’t know” — this is the wisdom of humility.
  • ★The poem follows the ABCB rhyme scheme (only the 2nd and 4th lines of each stanza rhyme).
  • ★Key rhyming pairs: well/shell, other/mother, find/blind, labours/neighbours.
  • ★For exams, be ready to explain what the bird thought at each stage and what the final lesson is.
Chapter 7
Change of Heart
Unit 4: Sports and Wellness — A story about a boy who learns that the true joy of sport lies in playing, not just winning.
📖 Summary

Prabhat was obsessed with winning. He refused to play any game where he might lose and only participated in sports he knew he could dominate. His entire sense of self-worth was tied to victory.

A new student named Surya joined the school. Surya was an excellent badminton player. During their Friday match, Prabhat kept close watch on the score. When the game got tight, Prabhat secretly changed the scoreboard to show himself winning. He won the match — but felt guilty inside.

What struck Prabhat was Surya’s reaction to losing. Surya didn’t mind at all — he smiled, congratulated Prabhat, and moved on to play basketball. Surya was terrible at basketball but played with obvious joy and enthusiasm, laughing at his own mistakes.

Watching Surya play badly but happily, Prabhat had a revelation: the true joy of sport is in the playing, not the winning. He changed his attitude and developed genuine sporting spirit — a real change of heart.

👤 Characters
  • ●Prabhat — A boy obsessed with winning who refused to play games he might lose. He cheated in a match but eventually developed true sporting spirit.
  • ●Surya — A new student and excellent badminton player who played every game with a smile, whether he won or lost. He embodied true sporting spirit.
  • ●The Coach — The school coach who organised Friday matches for the students.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: Sporting spirit and fair play. The story contrasts Prabhat’s win-at-all-costs attitude with Surya’s joyful, accepting approach to sport.

Moral: True joy in sport comes from participation and fair play, not just from winning. A real sportsperson plays with honesty and enjoys the game regardless of the result.

Sub-themes: Integrity, guilt and conscience, learning from others, the difference between a winner and a champion.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
DefeatLoss in a game or contest
On the contraryThe opposite of what was expected
TerribleExtremely bad; awful
SensedFelt or perceived without being told directly
SignificantImportant; having meaning
ConductedOrganised and carried out
PhenomenonA remarkable person or event
HopelessVery bad at something; without skill
Kept a close watch onMonitored carefully; watched attentively
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Past Perfect Tense: Formed with had + past participle — “Prabhat had changed the scoreboard before anyone noticed.” Used for actions completed before another past action.
  • ✎Identifying Tenses: Practice circling verbs in sentences and identifying whether they are Simple Past, Past Continuous or Past Perfect.
  • ✎Phrasal Expressions: Kept a close watch on (monitored), on the contrary (opposite), change of heart (change in attitude). Learn their meanings and usage.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★Prabhat’s flaw was his obsession with winning — he even cheated by changing the scoreboard.
  • ★Surya was the role model — he played every game with joy, whether he was good at it or not.
  • ★The turning point: watching Surya play basketball badly but happily made Prabhat realise sport is about enjoyment.
  • ★Key grammar: Past Perfect tense (had + past participle) — understand when and why it is used.
  • ★For value-based questions, focus on sporting spirit, fair play and honesty.
Chapter 8
The Winner
Unit 4: Sports and Wellness — A poem by Georgia Heard about children playing ball by a creek until night arrives and “wins” the game.
📖 Summary

Children are playing ball in the evening by a creek in their neighbour’s field. They run so fast that the speaker can’t catch her breath. The game is full of energy and joy.

As evening deepens, the “blue dark” turns to black. The cold grass begins to ache their bare feet. The trees seem to creep closer in the darkness.

The game is over — not because someone scored the final point, but because night has arrived. In the poet’s playful personification, “Night wins!”

📌 Key Points
  • ●Form: 3 stanzas in free verse with some visual patterns (staircase arrangement to show breathlessness).
  • ●The “Winner”: Not a player — it’s Night itself, personified as the ultimate winner who ends all games.
  • ●Setting: A creek in a neighbour’s field, evening turning to night.
  • ●Tone: Playful, nostalgic, slightly wistful as the game ends.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: The joy of childhood play and the passage of time. Night, as a personified force, always arrives to end the fun — time waits for no one.

Moral: Childhood play is precious but fleeting. Time (represented by night) always moves forward, reminding us to cherish every moment of joy.

Sub-themes: Personification of nature, the innocence of outdoor play, the transition from day to night as a metaphor for change.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
CreekA small stream or narrow waterway
BreathThe air taken in and out of the lungs
CreepTo move slowly and quietly; to approach gradually
AcheA dull, continuous pain
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Personification: Night is treated as a person who “wins” the game; trees “creep close.” Identify how non-human things are given human actions.
  • ✎Visual Poetry: The staircase arrangement of words on the page mimics breathlessness and running. Notice how the shape of the poem adds meaning.
  • ✎Water Bodies Vocabulary: Creek, river, lake, pond, stream, ocean, sea, brook, canal, reservoir. Know the differences between these water bodies.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The “winner” of the poem is Night — not any player. Night ends the game by making it too dark to play.
  • ★The poem uses personification: “Night wins” and “trees creep close” give human qualities to non-human things.
  • ★The poem is in free verse — it does not follow a regular rhyme or metre pattern.
  • ★The visual layout (staircase pattern) of some lines represents breathlessness from running.
  • ★A creek is a small stream — know the vocabulary for different water bodies.
Chapter 9
Kalakritiyon ka Bharat
Unit 5: Culture and Tradition — Children from different Indian states share their regional art forms through the “Ek Bharat Shreshtha Bharat” programme.
📖 Summary

Through the “Ek Bharat Shreshtha Bharat” programme, children from different states come together to share their regional art forms, showcasing India’s incredible artistic diversity.

Akanksha (Uttarakhand) presents Aipan folk art — traditional designs made with geru (red earth) and rice flour paste painted on brick-red walls. These are created during festivals and special occasions.

Priyaranjan (Odisha) demonstrates Dhokra metal craft — a 4,000-year-old art form that uses the lost-wax technique to create metal figurines and artefacts.

Chitra (Kerala) showcases coconut shell craft — beautiful, biodegradable items made from discarded coconut shells, turning waste into art.

Balamurali (Andhra Pradesh) presents Kondapalli wooden toys — light wooden toys painted with vegetable dyes and assembled using makku paste (a mixture of tamarind seed powder and sawdust).

👤 Characters
  • ●Akanksha — From Uttarakhand; presents Aipan folk art using geru and rice flour paste.
  • ●Priyaranjan — From Odisha; demonstrates the 4,000-year-old Dhokra metal craft.
  • ●Chitra — From Kerala; showcases biodegradable coconut shell craft.
  • ●Balamurali — From Andhra Pradesh; presents Kondapalli wooden toys painted with vegetable dyes.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: Unity in diversity — India’s rich cultural heritage expressed through its regional art forms. Each state has unique crafts that reflect local traditions, materials and creativity.

Moral: India’s diversity in art is a source of national pride. Knowing and respecting each other’s traditions strengthens our unity.

Sub-themes: Preservation of traditional crafts, sustainable and eco-friendly art (coconut shell craft, vegetable dyes), the “Ek Bharat Shreshtha Bharat” initiative.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
GeruRed earth; natural pigment used in traditional art
CarveTo cut a shape from wood, stone or other hard material
ScrapWaste material; leftover pieces
BiodegradableCapable of being broken down naturally by living organisms
TamarindA tropical fruit with a sour taste, used in food and crafts
SawdustFine wood particles produced when cutting or sanding wood
DyesSubstances used to colour materials
EnamelA glossy, protective coating applied to surfaces
📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Sequencing Words: Words that show order in a process — to begin, first, next, then, after that, finally. Used when describing how crafts are made step by step.
  • ✎Present Tense with Pronouns: Practice using present tense verbs with different pronouns — I paint, she carves, they mould, he dips, we create.
  • ✎Process Writing: Describing how something is made using sequencing words: “First, the wax model is made. Next, it is covered with clay. Then, molten metal is poured in.”
💡 Remember Points
  • ★Four art forms: Aipan (Uttarakhand), Dhokra (Odisha), Coconut shell craft (Kerala), Kondapalli toys (Andhra Pradesh).
  • ★Dhokra is the oldest art form mentioned — it is 4,000 years old and uses the lost-wax technique.
  • ★Kondapalli toys use makku paste (tamarind seed powder + sawdust) to join parts, and vegetable dyes for painting.
  • ★Coconut shell craft from Kerala is notable for being biodegradable and eco-friendly.
  • ★For exams, know which art form belongs to which state and the key materials/techniques used in each.
Chapter 10
The Kites
Unit 5: Culture and Tradition — A poem by Daphne Lister about a child watching colourful kites and imagining flying high above the world.
📖 Summary

A child watches colourful kites soaring in the sky, describing them as “coloured birds in the wind-whipped sky.” The sight fills the child with wonder and a deep longing to join them.

The child wishes to be “small and light as air” so that they could climb on a kite and sail up into the sky. The child imagines drifting on paper wings, hearing the songs of the wild wind.

From high above, the child dreams of looking down over the park and rooftops. People below would stand and stare, wishing they could be the child “high, high in the air.”

The poem captures the magic of kite-flying and the power of imagination to give us freedom to dream and explore beyond our physical limits.

📌 Key Points
  • ●Form: 5 stanzas with an ABCB rhyme scheme.
  • ●Speaker: A child watching kites and imagining flying with them.
  • ●Literary devices: Similes (“like coloured birds”, “light as air”) and alliteration (“wild wind”, “stand and stare”) throughout.
  • ●Tone: Joyful, dreamy, imaginative and free.
🌟 Theme & Moral

Theme: The power of imagination and the freedom of dreams. Kites symbolise the human desire to soar above limitations and see the world from new heights.

Moral: Imagination gives us the freedom to dream and explore. Even when we cannot fly physically, our minds can take us anywhere.

Sub-themes: Childhood wonder, the beauty of kite-flying as a cultural activity, the joy of outdoor play, the connection between nature (wind) and human joy.

📚 Key Vocabulary
WordMeaning
Wind-whippedBlown strongly by the wind
DriftTo float or move gently through the air
SailTo move smoothly through the air or water
Paper wingsThe kite’s surface, imagined as wings for flying

📝 Grammar Focus
  • ✎Simile: A comparison using “like” or “as” — “like coloured birds” (kites compared to birds), “light as air” (the child wishes to be weightless). Identify and create your own similes.
  • ✎Alliteration: Repetition of initial consonant sounds — “wild wind”, “stand and stare.” These create rhythm and emphasis in the poem.
  • ✎Kite Vocabulary: Parts of a kite — nose, spine, cover, line, tail, reel. Know the name and function of each part.
  • ✎Cinquain Poem: A 5-line poem with a specific syllable pattern (2-4-6-8-2). Practice writing cinquain poems about kites or nature.
💡 Remember Points
  • ★The poem uses similes (“like coloured birds”, “light as air”) and alliteration (“wild wind”, “stand and stare”).
  • ★The poem has 5 stanzas with an ABCB rhyme scheme.
  • ★The child imagines drifting on paper wings, hearing songs of the wild wind, and looking down at parks and rooftops.
  • ★Parts of a kite: nose (front tip), spine (vertical stick), cover (paper/cloth surface), line (string), tail (trailing strip), reel (spool for the string).
  • ★For exams, be ready to identify similes and alliteration in the poem and explain their effect.
